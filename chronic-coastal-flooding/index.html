Chronic coastal flooding tolerance in rural North Carolina

How are people dealing with increasing chronic flooding risk from higher tides and more intense rainfall?

James P. Collins, Ryan McCune

Chronic coastal flooding, sometimes called "high-tide flooding" or "sunny day flooding," is when water comes onto land outside of a major event, like a storm surge from a hurricane (O'Donnell et al. 2024; Gold et al. 2023). While sometimes seen as only a nuisance, chronic coastal flooding can disrupt daily life, cause economic losses, and damage property (Hino et al. 2019; Moftakhari et al. 2017).

To understand how chronic coastal flooding is already impacting rural communities, researchers in the Sunny Day Flooding Project are studying a collection of communities in eastern Carteret County, North Carolina, known as Down East, North River, Merrimon, and South River.

We have placed five real-time flooding sensors throughout Carteret County. Four sensors are placed Down East (North River Bridge, Davis, Sea Level, and Cedar Island stretch) and one in Beaufort (Front Street). These sensors take photos of street conditions and measure water level at the road. We've used these sensors to estimate the number of days roads have been inundated by chronic coastal flooding.

Additionally, in June and July 2024 we interviewed people familiar with these areas about their experiences of chronic coastal flooding. We also asked people how they are dealing with it at home, work, and in their communities. We conducted 16 interviews with residents and six interviews with "local experts" in education, agriculture, parks and conservation, public safety, and utilities.

Altogether, we've found that (1) chronic coastal flooding is impacting communities now, (2) people are accommodating current risk and can tolarate more flooding, and (3) flooding risk tolerance is entangled with voluntary immobility.
